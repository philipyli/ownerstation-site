  2. Staging

    Help buyers imagine their new life at your home.

    Staging means putting your daily personal items away, then dressing up your home to appeal to the widest range of buyers. This may include cleaning, decluttering, redecorating, painting, & landscaping. In some neighborhoods, your stager may even ask you to move out, then move in their furniture to make your rooms look larger.

    Should I hire a pro?

    Most sellers get thousands more by hiring a stager. You can replicate most of a professional's work yourself if so inclined. However, a stager's sense of what styles appeal to local buyers may prove valuable. Also, it often takes a fresh eye to notice the clutter in your own home.

    Get Started

    When evaluating stagers, ask for addresses of recently sold homes they staged. Hire a staging consultant for $300-500, furniture rental for $3000+, or enlist a trusted friend with great design sense to help.

  3. Photos

    Stand out online with professional photos.

    Photography is more than about having a fancy camera. It takes experience to master to the right lenses, filters, & lighting to brighten dark corners and bring out shadows. Those who work in real estate know how to accentuate specific features of your home to maximize marketability.

    Why hire a pro?

    Good photography can dramatically increase the number of viewings to your listing online, leading to more buyers to your showings & more offers for your home. As DSLR cameras become popular, photography services have also gone down in price.

    Get Started

    When evaluating photographers, ask for addresses of recently sold homes they photographed. A photographer might charge $300-500, or you may enlist photo retouching help for around $50.

  4. Pricing

    Engage buyers all at once with a well-informed opening price.

    Your listing will get the most attention on its first week. Buyers tend to avoid new listings they consider overpriced, but may see a later price reduction as a signal to ask for an even lower price. Set a reasonable opening price to engage potential buyers all at the same time, setting them up to compete for your home.

    How much is my home worth?

    Estimates on sites like Zillow, Epprasial, & Homegain can be misleading, but they do affect buyer expectations. When pricing your home, compile a list of recently sold comparable homes in your neighborhood & consider intangibles such as views & remodeling.

    Get Started

    For added confidence, get an appraiser's objective opinion. They see more local transactions than anyone else. And, since their business comes mostly from banks, they may have an idea of what pricing lenders are likely to support.

  5. Disclosures

    Secure your transaction with disclosure paperwork.

    Disclosure laws give buyers the right to sue you if they find hidden problems in your home after they move in. Disclosing problems beforehand protects you from that risk & assures buyers in making offers. By being as thorough as possible in your disclosures, buyers are also less likely to cancel their offer or ask for a price reduction due to problems uncovered in their inspections.

    How do I best protect myself?

    The best way to meet disclosure obligations is to conduct your own pre-sale inspection. State and local laws specify what disclosure forms you need to complete. Your local inspector can help you understand those forms & make informed responses.

  6. Listing

    Reach buyers on major listing sites.

    92% of buyers search online for homes. Listing sites like Zillow & Trulia allow you to list for free. You can post to many listing sites at once using a free syndication service such as Postlets. Craigslist requires you to post manually & renew your listing continuously.


    Should I list on MLS or get premium placement?

    Before the rise of search sites, the only way to reach a large number of buyers was to ask an agent to put your home in your local Multiple Listing Service (MLS). If you'd like your home to be found on Redfin & Realtor.com, you still need to post on MLS. If you're in an neighborhood with a large number of competing listings, premium placement on major search sites can help you stand out & maximize inquiries.

  7. Showings

    Make the best impression to as many buyers as possible.

    Open houses are not expected in every market, but a crowd generates competitive excitement for your home. Most visitors might be just curious, but any one of them could know someone actively looking for a home like yours. If you're emotional about the prospect of leaving your home, ask a trusted relative or friend to host instead.


    How do I run an open house?

    Be aware of customary open house times in your neighborhood. Capture buyers with signs at strategic intersections. Have a stack of color printouts of your listing handy, and a binder with your disclosures for all to see. Maintain a sign-up list, and follow up with visitors to answer any questions. By treating everyone you meet seriously, you encourage offers with your professionalism.

  8. Contract

    Review buyers & handle offers like a pro.

    Ask all buyers for a loan pre-approval letter from a bank or proof of funds in conjunction with any offer. Offers should also be accompanied by a deposit check payable to a title or escrow company. If you're anticipating multiple offers, it may make sense to set a deadline. Be clear whether you want offers emailed, couriered, or delivered in person.



    What should I pay attention to?

    Agents tend to use standard offer forms by the state or local Realtor Association. Look for any contingencies (loan, inspection), and make sure you're okay with the timeframes specified. Once you accept an offer or counteroffer, have the buyer sign your disclosures on any inspection reports.
